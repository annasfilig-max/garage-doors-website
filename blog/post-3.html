What to Look For When Hiring a Garage Door Contractor

By [Author Name] February 28, 2026 7 min read

Not all garage door contractors are equal. The industry has a low barrier to entry — a van, some tools, and a website — which means the gap between excellent and poor service is wide, and the consequences of choosing wrong can range from shoddy work to genuine safety hazards.

This guide walks you through exactly how to vet a contractor before you hire, covering credentials, pricing red flags, and the specific questions that separate professional operators from fly-by-night outfits.

Start with Licensing and Insurance

This is the non-negotiable foundation. Before you discuss price or scheduling, ask directly: "Are you licensed in [State] and do you carry liability insurance and workers' comp?"

Here's why each matters:

  • State contractor's license: Licensing requirements vary by state, but where required, a license means the contractor has passed relevant tests, met experience thresholds, and is accountable to a licensing board. You can verify a license number through your state's contractor licensing database — take five minutes to do it.
  • General liability insurance: If a technician damages your home or vehicle during the job, their liability coverage pays for it — not your homeowner's policy. Without it, you may be pursuing an individual for damages with no real recourse.
  • Workers' compensation: If a technician is injured on your property and the company carries workers' comp, it's handled through their policy. Without it, you could potentially be liable for an injured worker's medical costs — depending on your state's laws.

Any legitimate contractor will provide proof of insurance without hesitation. If they deflect, get evasive, or pressure you to move forward without it — that's your answer.

Read Reviews Carefully — Not Just the Star Rating

A 4.8-star average across 200 reviews tells a different story than a 4.8 across 12. Volume matters. Look for contractors with a substantial number of reviews on multiple platforms — Google, Yelp, Facebook — and read a sample of them, including the most recent ones and any negative reviews.

"How a company responds to negative reviews tells you more about their character than the negative review itself."

Look for patterns in both positive and negative feedback. Consistent mentions of "showed up on time," "price matched the quote," and "cleaned up after themselves" are reliable indicators of a professional operation. Repeated complaints about "added charges at the end" or "different tech than quoted" are equally reliable warning signs.

Pricing Red Flags to Watch For

Garage door pricing is relatively transparent for most common services, which makes unusual pricing practices easy to spot once you know what to look for:

  • Unusually low phone quotes: A quote that seems remarkably low is often designed to get a technician through your door. Once on-site, they diagnose a more expensive problem — which may or may not be real. Get at least two quotes to establish a baseline.
  • Large upfront payment required: Standard practice is payment upon completion. A demand for 50% or more upfront before any work begins — especially from a company you can't verify — is a significant red flag.
  • Verbal-only quotes: Any professional contractor will provide a written, itemized estimate. If a quote is only given verbally, ask for it in writing before approving work. This protects you from disputes about what was agreed.
  • Pressure to decide immediately: "I can give you this price today only" is a sales tactic, not a service indicator. A legitimate company doesn't need to pressure you into a same-day decision.

Questions to Ask Before You Book

A few direct questions will quickly reveal whether you're dealing with a professional operation:

  • Can you provide your license number and proof of insurance before we schedule?
  • Will the same technician who gives the estimate do the work, or will it be subcontracted?
  • What warranty do you offer on parts and labor?
  • How do you handle the situation if a different problem is discovered once work begins?
  • Can I see some examples of similar jobs you've completed in my area?

A contractor who answers these questions confidently and directly — without deflection or irritation — has passed the first filter. One who becomes evasive about any of them should be reconsidered.

Local vs. National

Both locally owned companies and national franchises can do good work, but they operate differently in ways worth understanding. A national franchise offers standardized processes and brand accountability. A local company often offers faster response, direct access to decision-makers, and deeper community reputation — but quality varies more widely.

For a locally owned company, ask how long they've been operating in your specific area, not just "how long have you been in business." A company that's been serving [Service Region] for 15 years has a local reputation to protect. A company that opened its local branch last year may not.

The Bottom Line

Vetting a contractor takes 15 minutes and can save you hundreds of dollars, a bad installation, or a liability headache. Verify the license, confirm the insurance, read the reviews for patterns, get a written quote, and ask the questions above. Most legitimate contractors will welcome the due diligence — it tells them they're dealing with a serious homeowner.

If any step of that process is met with resistance or pressure, trust your instincts and move on. There are plenty of qualified, honest contractors in every market. You deserve one of them.
