How to Get the Most Life Out of Your Garage Door

By [Author Name] March 15, 2026 6 min read

A well-maintained garage door should last 15 to 30 years, depending on the materials, climate, and usage. Yet the majority of doors we service as technicians are failing years ahead of schedule — not because of bad materials or bad luck, but because of skipped maintenance that costs almost nothing to do.

The good news: garage door maintenance is genuinely simple. You don't need mechanical expertise, special tools, or an afternoon to spare. Twice a year — spring and fall are ideal — you can run through a short checklist that will add years to your door's working life and catch small problems before they become expensive ones.

The Twice-Yearly Maintenance Checklist

Set a calendar reminder and work through these items each time. The whole checklist takes 20 to 30 minutes:

  • Visual inspection from the inside: With the door closed, look at the springs, cables, rollers, and tracks from inside the garage. You're looking for obvious damage — fraying, rust, cracks, or sections that look bent or misaligned. If you spot a broken spring or frayed cable, don't operate the door — call a technician.
  • Lubricate moving parts: Apply a garage door-specific lubricant (not WD-40, which is a cleaner, not a lubricant) to the rollers, hinges, tracks, and springs. This is the single most impactful maintenance task you can perform. Metal-on-metal friction is the primary cause of premature wear across all door components.
  • Test the balance: Disconnect the opener by pulling the red emergency release cord, then manually lift the door to about waist height and let go. A properly balanced door should hold its position or drift very slowly. If it drops or rises on its own, the springs need adjustment — call a technician.
  • Clean and inspect the tracks: Wipe out any debris, dirt, or buildup from the tracks with a damp cloth. Check that the tracks are properly aligned — they should be vertical on the sides and slightly angled back toward the rear at the top. Don't try to adjust tracks yourself; they require precise calibration.
  • Test the auto-reverse: Place a flat board (like a 2x4) flat on the ground in the door's path and activate close. The door should reverse when it contacts the board. If it doesn't, the close-force setting needs adjustment — call a technician, as this is a critical safety feature.
  • Check the weatherstripping: Inspect the rubber seal along the bottom of the door for cracking, gaps, or sections that have pulled away. Replace it if it's no longer making full contact with the floor — this is an inexpensive fix that prevents water intrusion and energy loss.
"Lubrication alone, done twice a year, will extend the life of your rollers, hinges, and springs by years. It's the easiest maintenance task and the most commonly skipped."

What Not to DIY

Home maintenance guides often encourage doing as much yourself as possible, but garage doors have a few areas where professional service is genuinely necessary — both for quality and safety:

  • Spring replacement or adjustment: Torsion springs and extension springs are under extreme tension. Improper handling has caused serious injuries. This is always a job for a licensed technician.
  • Cable replacement: Cables work in tandem with the springs and require the correct tension to function safely. Same rule applies — professional only.
  • Track realignment: Minor cleaning is fine, but bending or repositioning tracks requires experience to avoid creating new alignment problems.
  • Opener programming and sensor adjustment: While not dangerous in the same way as spring work, improper adjustment can defeat safety features. Have a technician handle anything beyond basic remote pairing.

The Annual Professional Inspection

Even with consistent DIY maintenance, an annual professional inspection is worth scheduling. A trained technician will check spring tension, cable wear, roller and hinge condition, and opener function in a way that catches issues you can't see with a visual inspection alone.

Think of it the same way you think about a car service interval: you can check the tires and top up fluids yourself, but you still want a mechanic to look at the brakes and the things underneath. The annual inspection is the equivalent of that service interval for your garage door.

Most inspections take under an hour, and most companies — including ours — include minor adjustments in the service call. It's a small investment that consistently pays for itself by catching wear before it becomes a failure.

Signs That Maintenance Has Been Missed Too Long

If you've inherited a home with an older door or moved in somewhere with an unmaintained system, watch for these signs that the door needs more than maintenance — it needs a service call:

  • Visible rust on springs or cables
  • Rollers that are cracked, flat on one side, or wobbling in their brackets
  • Cables that are frayed, kinked, or have jumped off a drum
  • Sections of track that are visibly bent or pulling away from the wall

In these cases, a maintenance session won't be enough. Have a technician assess the system before you run it extensively — continuing to operate a door with these issues accelerates the damage and increases repair cost.

The bottom line: a few minutes twice a year makes an enormous difference in how long your garage door lasts and how reliably it operates. Build the habit now and you'll get decades of trouble-free service out of your investment.
